> [!Definition] Definition
> Frame is not a shape or one particular tension level, it describes the mechanism through which movement information travels from one person to another. 

Most "initiating" information flows from the leader to the follower, but information flows both directions constantly. 

Bracing (or active tension) for our body means protection. It is expecting something that could go wrong, something that could be dangerous. [^2]  ^600607

# Position of Frame %% fold %% 
We want to have our elbows to be wide (wider then the position of hands). hands mostly paralel to the ground. 
If we move our elbows closer to the body (in the "inner circle"), it will mostly make our hands rotate up and distort the impulse. 
If we move our elbows too wide, it will make our shoulders [[Upper back backbend#Shoulder position is important too|internally rotate]] which moves the shoulder blade further away from the spine and reduces the stability of the whole back. Therefore breaking up the connection between the arm and the back. Also it can cause the chest to collapse and feel closed. 

Keeping allows and wrist flexible so they allow the energy to pass through them and work also as some sort of cushion. Keeping a slight tension to avoid collapse of frame. 

Focus on guiding your energy from center rather then from your arms. 





The goal is generally to have the minimum level of tension at any given time to facilitate a smooth flow of information between dancers - which can change depending on the forces and directions involved. However a higher or lower level of tension - while it wouldn't be the most "efficient" generally speaking - could create a different set of movement possibilities than the more efficient frame, so choosing to engage to a greater or lesser degree may have advantages (especially if it's done intentionally for that reason). I always teach minimal necessary tension as default because it's easier to add some tension situationally than to remove some. ([[Zouk nerds podcast]])
